## Article 3: "I Tracked Every Random Thought for 30 Days. Here's What I Found."

- **File**: `blog/30-day-thought-experiment.html`
- **Target keywords**: thought tracking experiment, brain dump results, what happens when you write everything down
- **Audience**: Curious self-improvement readers, experiment lovers
- **Tone**: Personal experiment, data-driven storytelling
- **Word count**: ~1000

### Outline
1. **Hook**: What if you wrote down every random thought for a month? I did. The results surprised me.
2. **The setup**: Rules — capture everything, no filtering, no judging. Phone always nearby.
3. **The numbers**: 847 thoughts captured. Breakdown:
   - 40% tasks I would have forgotten (bills, errands, follow-ups)
   - 25% anxieties that needed processing (not action, just acknowledgment)
   - 20% ideas worth exploring (side projects, career moves, gifts)
   - 15% noise (random observations, passing thoughts)
4. **The surprise**: The anxieties category. Writing them down made them smaller. Most never needed action — just visibility.
5. **The real insight**: 85% of my random thoughts were actually useful. I was losing most of them before this experiment.
6. **What changed**: I stopped trusting my memory and started trusting a system.
7. **Soft CTA**: "I built Drift because of this experiment." + link

## Article 4: "Why Your To-Do List Makes You Feel Worse, Not Better"

- **File**: `blog/todo-list-guilt.html`
- **Target keywords**: to-do list anxiety, to-do list guilt, why to-do lists don't work
- **Audience**: Chronic list-makers who feel guilty about unchecked items
- **Tone**: Empathetic, slightly provocative
- **Word count**: ~800

### Outline
1. **Hook**: Your to-do list has 47 items. You'll do 3 today. The other 44 will stare at you, judging.
2. **The guilt loop**: Add task → don't finish → feel bad → add more to compensate → feel worse.
3. **The design flaw**: To-do lists treat everything as equal. "Buy milk" sits next to "figure out career direction." Your brain can't prioritize a flat list.
4. **What research says**: Zeigarnik effect — unfinished tasks occupy mental bandwidth. A long to-do list is literally draining your cognitive resources.
5. **The alternative**: Instead of listing everything to do, ask: "What's the ONE thing that matters today?" Let the rest exist somewhere — just not in your face.
6. **The system**: Capture everything (brain dump) → surface what matters (AI/rules) → hide the rest (not delete, just park).
7. **Soft CTA**: "Drift does exactly this — you dump everything, it shows you what matters today." + link

## Article 5: "The Brain Dump Method: Empty Your Head in 2 Minutes, Focus for Hours"

- **File**: `blog/brain-dump-method.html`
- **Target keywords**: brain dump method, how to brain dump, clear your mind technique, mental clarity technique
- **Audience**: People searching for practical focus techniques
- **Tone**: How-to, actionable, practical
- **Word count**: ~800

### Outline
1. **Hook**: Your brain is a terrible storage device. It's an incredible processing device. Stop using it for storage.
2. **What is a brain dump**: Write down everything in your head — tasks, worries, ideas, random thoughts — in one go. No filtering. 2 minutes.
3. **Step-by-step**:
   - Step 1: Set a 2-minute timer
   - Step 2: Write everything (phone, paper, app — doesn't matter)
   - Step 3: Don't organize. Don't judge. Just dump.
   - Step 4: Look at the list. Circle the top 3 things that matter TODAY.
   - Step 5: Do those 3. The rest waits.
4. **Why it works**: Externalizing thoughts frees working memory (cognitive offloading). Your brain stops background-processing stored items.
5. **When to do it**: Morning (start clear), before bed (sleep better), when overwhelmed (instant relief).
6. **Soft CTA**: "I built an app around this method — Drift helps you brain dump and then uses AI to surface what matters." + link

- **Description**: Your brain is a terrible storage device. Here's how to empty it in 2 minutes and focus for hours.
